“Make Every Day Matter”
in the Early Years Classroom

Having 25 small people entrusted to me for a year of their lives is a both a responsibility and a privilege. I know how important the elementary years are in the development of caring, knowledgeable and responsible adults. I believe that my relationship with them is instrumental in modeling the learner profiles and an optimistic belief in each child now and for their future. I like to encourage independence skills in my students and a sense of ownership and pride in their own and other’s work.

My personal philosophy is “to make everyday in the classroom matter” and putting lots of effort into planning, collaboration and reflection makes this a reality. To this end, I also use the standards and expectations of the school curriculum and give the children deliberate, meaningful learning opportunities to enable them to incrementally meet these standards and regularly monitor their progress. Routines are also important for class life and ‘little bits of rich learning’ can be included in the daily rituals and transitions.

I have a developmental approach to assessing each child’s skills and learning needs. I also know that students have different thinking styles, interests and backgrounds. Taking regular observations, photos and anecdotal records provides valuable evidence for the above and enables me to differentiate my teaching. They are also valuable for keeping parents regularly in touch with their child’s life in the classroom.

We all enjoy communicating and being seen and heard for the interesting people we all are. I encourage opportunities for every child to communicate who they are, as well as what they think, wonder, see and feel. People are also naturally interested in each other and the classroom is a fertile place for learning from peers and developing valuable friendships and social skills. In the early years this is of particular importance as children broaden their social circle from family to include  classmates and teachers.

I love stories, poems, songs, visuals, puppets and books and I use these daily to:

  • frontload information, ideas, perspectives for units.
  • model reading strategies and a love of reading and books
  • show ‘mentor texts’ (work from a single author/illustrator/genre) to model and analyze motivations, conventions and characteristics of writing and writers.
  • raise specific issues (e.g. changes in family life or class life issues)
  • broaden my students perspective and understanding of the world through story telling

In the early years, I teach mathematics in how it is meaningful and authentic to the child. I use number ‘stories’ and real examples every day, e.g. people in your family; how many teeth have you lost; birthdays; classroom times; patterns in the calendar and in all ways that numbers can relate to the inquiry units. I also use manipulatives in playful and structured ways to bridge the important step from concrete to abstract mathematical thinking. Playing math  games are an excellent way for practicing number skills and knowledge and learning from one another. I enjoy the challenge of teaching mathematics in all sorts of fun ways.

My personal interpretation of how the PYP works in my classroom is that there are six inquiry themes throughout the year on which all learning is based. They provide the background landscape on which experiences are had; questions are asked, stories are told and information shared. There is also an overarching generic ‘process’ that stipulates what happens at the beginning, middle and end of each Unit. It is also expected that as this ‘process of inquiry’ unfolds in the classroom it will also ‘inform’ itself. Earlier decisions may be renegotiated or un-made decisions are left open, until closer to the time that they are needed to be made. I found that this sense ‘not knowing in advance’ (although vaguely uncomfortable) always kept me listening out for clues and sign posts in the class discussions as to ‘what, or where to go next? In this way, the choices the children make are able to be included and the dynamic and responsive nature of inquiry learning is supported.

Here are some ‘notes to self’ I made on how to support the ‘nature of inquiry’:

  • model curiosity: if I do ‘wonder’, say so
  • give open-ended experiences (where choices and outcomes are broad)
  • give challenges (where the choices are deliberately limited);
  • a little bit of fun goes a long-learning-way
  • encourage, no, expect reflection and thinking time regularly
  • keep everything simple (some of the most treasured learning experiences have simple beginnings)
  • listen for any connections being made, and voice them
  • don’t lose faith if they ‘don’t seem to get it yet’ - every bit does count, we only see the tip of the ‘what am I learning?’ iceberg
  • these early years are a lot about developing the ‘learning self’ rather than finding or knowing the right answers
  • admire: constantly be amazed at what they can do already and they’ve only been on this planet for a few short years!

My experience is that a school is an intense environment with multiple types of professional relationships, roles and demands. As a teacher, I am careful to be respectful and professional to all I meet, and to stay aware of when I need to invest more time and energy into my work and colleagues. I also know when it is appropriate to pace myself and pull back and prioritize my time and energies so that I can make every day matter for my students in the classroom.
